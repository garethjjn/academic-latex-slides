# Contribution-Statement Formulas

**Source.** Reverse-engineered from the contributions paragraphs of 6 corpus introductions. Use these as templates — adapt the bracketed slots to your specific paper.

---

## The 8 contribution formulas

### Formula 1 — "Add to literature L by mechanism M" (most common, used 14× across corpus)

```
[Ordinal], we add to the [growing/extensive] literature that examines [TOPIC] by [WHAT WE DO].
```

**Examples from corpus:**
- "First, we add to the literature that examines cross-country differences in earnings' value relevance." (07-DHT)
- "Second, our study adds to the recent stream of research that examines the impact of institutional factors on capital market development..." (07-DHT)
- "Third, our findings contribute to the extensive literature that examines the market response to earnings announcements." (07-DHT)
- "First, we add to a growing literature on the role of individual audit partners in the audit process (see Lennox and Wu (2018) for a review)." (26-KLYY)
- "Second, we add to an emerging literature on the role of culture in accounting and finance." (26-KLYY)

**When to use:** Default option. Every paper gets at least 2 of these.

---

### Formula 2 — "Contribute to literature L on dimension D" (used 8×)

```
Our study contributes to the [auditing/accounting/finance] literature [on TOPIC] by examining [DIMENSION OR ANGLE].
```

**Examples:**
- "Our study contributes to the auditing literature by examining the trade-off between improved auditor competence and reduced auditor independence." (24-DLWW)
- "Our paper contributes to the literature in several ways." (07-DHT) — opening of multi-contribution sequence

**When to use:** When you want to lead with a specific *angle* or *trade-off*, not just identify the literature.

---

### Formula 3 — "We are the first to examine X" (used 4× — DeFond signature)

```
[We are the first to / Our study is the first to] [VERB] [PRECISE PHENOMENON].
```

**Examples:**
- "Specifically, we are the first to test whether engagement auditors with tax expertise, who do not also provide non-audit tax services, reduce tax aggressiveness." (25-DQSZ)
- "Thus, we identify an audit quality determinant that is new to the literature: auditors' social networks." (24-DLWW)
- "While most studies of auditor expertise examine its effect on audit quality, we examine the effect of individual auditors' tax expertise on clients' tax aggressiveness." (25-DQSZ)
- "To our knowledge this is the first study in the accounting literature to use international earnings announcement dates from the I/B/E/S database." (07-DHT)

**When to use:** When the contribution is genuinely a **first-mover** result — must be defensible. Pair with a literature-mapping clause to show you know what came before. Reviewers WILL google this claim.

---

### Formula 4 — "Complement / extend literature on Y" (used 6×)

```
Our findings [complement/extend] [the broader literature on TOPIC / a related literature on Y] by [WHAT WE ADD].
```

**Examples:**
- "We also complement the broader literature on social connections by identifying a setting in which a financial intermediary's social connections can lead to favorable economic outcomes." (24-DLWW)
- "Our finding complements Li et al. (2020) who document that embedded financial analysts also have informational advantage in forecasting connected firms' earnings in China." (24-DLWW)
- "Our study also extends the literature which finds that audit partner characteristics help explain the variation in audit outcomes (Gul et al., 2013)." (25-DQSZ)
- "We extend this literature by ... " (26-KLYY, 07-DHT)

**When to use:** When the contribution piggybacks on a related literature without being central to it. Lower-stakes than Formula 2.

---

### Formula 5 — "Implications for [audience]" (used 5×, often as final contribution)

```
[Our findings / Our study also] has implications for [REGULATORS / PRACTITIONERS / FUTURE RESEARCH] [in DOMAIN].
```

**Examples:**
- "Our study also has implications for future auditing research." (24-DLWW)
- "Our study also has implications for relation-based economies, such as China." (24-DLWW)
- "Our findings should be of interest to auditors who actively manage client risk and to standard-setters who recently dropped conservatism as a desired attribute of financial reporting quality." (16-DLZ — abstract, but same pattern)

**When to use:** As the final numbered contribution, especially if the paper has policy or regulatory bite. Don't overuse — 1 implications paragraph max.

---

### Formula 6 — "Fill a gap in literature L" (used 3×)

```
[Ordinal], we fill a gap in the literature that examines [TOPIC]. [Specifically/Prior studies are unable to address this question because [GAP]].
```

**Examples:**
- "First, we fill a gap in the literature that examines the effect of auditors' tax expertise on clients' tax aggressiveness." (25-DQSZ)
- "Prior studies are unable to address this question because the engagement auditors they study also prepare and sign their audit clients' tax returns..." (25-DQSZ continuation)

**When to use:** Specific, defensible gap. Pair with a sentence explaining WHY prior studies couldn't address it (institutional / data limitation / setting confound).

---

### Formula 7 — "We use China's institutional features to identify X" (China-specific, used 3×)

```
[Ordinal], we add to the growing line of studies that use China's [unique institutional features / distinctive setting] to examine research questions that are difficult to address in other settings.
```

**Examples:**
- "Third, we also add to the growing line of studies that use China's unique institutional features to examine research questions that are difficult to address in other settings." (25-DQSZ)
- "While a major challenge in the auditor expertise literature is the ability to identify convincing proxies, particularly at the audit partner level, China's distinctive setting allows us to identify a novel measure of partners' tax expertise..." (25-DQSZ)

**When to use:** Always, when using Chinese data. This contribution paragraph **anticipates** the reviewer's complaint that "this is just a China paper" by reframing China as a methodological asset, not a limitation.

---

### Formula 8 — "Methodological contribution: novel measure / proxy" (used 4×)

```
We [develop / construct / introduce] [the concept of / a novel measure of / new empirical proxies for] [CONSTRUCT], based on [METHODOLOGY].
```

**Examples:**
- "First, we develop the concept and empirical proxies of auditor industry range based on the findings in psychology and cognitive science (Dane, 2010; Epstein, 2019; Kahneman and Klein, 2009)." (24-DHXZ)
- "We measure the cultural component of audit partners' trust beliefs (PartnerTrust) using a two-step procedure..." (26-KLYY — though this appears in design block, the contribution version of it is Formula 8)

**When to use:** When the paper introduces a measurable quantity new to the literature. This is high-value if the measure proves useful.

## How to combine the 8 formulas in one introduction

Pick **3–4 contributions** for an empirical paper. The corpus modal patterns:

### Pattern A — "Methods + 2 Lits + Implications" (used by 24-DLWW, 25-DQSZ)
```
1. [Formula 8: novel measure/setting]
2. [Formula 1 or 2: literature 1]
3. [Formula 1 or 2: literature 2]
4. [Formula 5: implications for X]
```

### Pattern B — "Direct + 3 Lits" (used by 07-DHT, 25-DQSZ)
```
1. [Formula 3: first to examine X] OR [Formula 6: fill gap]
2. [Formula 1: literature 1]
3. [Formula 1: literature 2]
4. [Formula 4: complement literature 3]
```

### Pattern C — "Compact 2-contribution" (used by 26-KLYY)
```
1. [Formula 1: literature on X]
2. [Formula 1: literature on Y]
```

Use this when the paper has a clear two-pronged contribution and you want a tight intro.

### Pattern D — "Conceptual + Empirical + Theoretical + Methodological" (used by 24-DHXZ)
```
1. [Formula 8: develop concept/measure]
2. [Formula 1: contribute to empirical literature]
3. [Formula 1: contribute to broader theoretical literature]
4. [Formula 4: complement methodological literature]
```

## Forbidden contribution formulas (NEVER use)

The following do NOT appear anywhere in the 6-paper corpus:

- ❌ "This paper sheds light on..."  (banned econ-write phrase, also banned in audit corpus)
- ❌ "This paper paves the way for..."
- ❌ "This is a pivotal contribution..."
- ❌ "Our findings are groundbreaking..."
- ❌ "We provide novel insight into..." (overused, hedge if you must)
- ❌ "Our findings are very important for [field]..."
- ❌ "We hope that future research will..." (use Formula 5 instead)
- ❌ Self-praise adjectives ("important", "novel", "rigorous") describing one's own work
- ❌ "To the best of our knowledge" (use the more confident "We are the first to..." with verifiable claim, or drop the claim)

The DeFond/Zuo style is **conservatively confident** — claims are precise enough to be falsifiable, no marketing adjectives.

## The closing limitation paragraph (optional 5th element)

In 4 of 6 corpus papers, after the contributions, there is a single paragraph that **acknowledges scope limits while reframing them as features**.

### Standard formula

```
We acknowledge, however, that [POTENTIAL LIMITATION]. In particular, [SPECIFIC NUANCE]. Nonetheless, [REFRAME AS FEATURE / SCOPE STATEMENT]. [Optional: forward-looking sentence about generalizability].
```

### Example (24-DLWW)

> "We acknowledge, however, that our use of Chinese data may limit the generalizability of our findings. In particular, because China's economy is traditionally relationship-based, the extent to which our findings extend to economies that rely more on arms-length contracting, such as the U.S., is unclear. Nonetheless, China is representative of many emerging and transitional economies that engage primarily in relationship-based contracting (Ball et al., 2000, 2003; Fan et al., 2011; Rajan and Zingales, 1998). Indeed, a recent PWC report predicts that by 2050, four of the top five largest economies in the world, based on purchasing-power adjusted GDP, will be emerging economies (China, India, Indonesia, and Brazil), with the U.S. being the sole developed economy."

### When to include this paragraph

- ✅ Using non-US data (especially China)
- ✅ Using a small or unusual sample
- ✅ Lacking a clean natural experiment
- ❌ Don't include if the paper has US data and a natural experiment — would look defensive

### When NOT to include limitation language

- During the contribution paragraphs themselves (don't undermine the contribution while making it)
- Anywhere with words like "we cannot fully rule out" or "we acknowledge that we may have missed" (these are conclusion-section moves, not introduction moves)
- More than once

## Cheat sheet: 1-paragraph contribution drafting workflow

Given the paper's main finding F and 3 reference literatures L1, L2, L3:

```
PASS 1 — Slot the literatures into formulas:
  L1 (closest competitor): use Formula 2 or Formula 3
  L2 (second-closest): use Formula 1
  L3 (broader / cross-disciplinary): use Formula 4
  Optional: implications for audience A using Formula 5

PASS 2 — Write each contribution as 3-4 sentences:
  Sentence 1: State what literature you contribute to.
  Sentence 2: What does that literature currently know? (cite 2-3 papers)
  Sentence 3: What does THIS paper add that the literature was missing?
  Sentence 4 (optional): One implication or extension.

PASS 3 — Audit the verbs against style_dna.md whitelist.
PASS 4 — Audit for forbidden formulas above.
PASS 5 — If using Chinese data, append the limitation paragraph.
```

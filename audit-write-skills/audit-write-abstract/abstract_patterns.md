# Audit-Paper Abstract: Move Anatomy and Sentence-Level Templates

**Source corpus.** 7 abstracts from JAE / TAR audit papers, 2007–2026:

- DeFond, Hung, Trezevant 2007 JAE — *Investor Protection and Earnings Announcements* (`07-DHT`)
- DeFond, Zhang 2014 JAE — *Archival Auditing Research* (`14-DZ`) — review article, abstract has different shape
- DeFond, Lim, Zang 2016 TAR — *Client Conservatism and Auditor-Client Contracting* (`16-DLZ`)
- DeFond, Li, Wong, Wu 2024 JAE — *Competence vs. Independence: Auditors' Connections* (`24-DLWW`) ★ closest analogue
- Dekeyser, He, Xiao, Zuo 2024 JAE — *Auditor Industry Range* (`24-DHXZ`) ★ Zuo lineage
- DeFond, Qi, Si, Zhang 2025 JAE — *Tax Expertise of Signatory Auditors* (`25-DQSZ`) ★ signatory-partner design
- Khurana, Li, Yeung, Yu 2026 JAE — *Audit Partners' Cultural Trust* (`26-KLYY`) ★ partner-trait paper

The 14-DZ review-paper abstract follows a distinct "we define / we provide / we urge" structure and is treated separately below — do not use it as a template for empirical abstracts.

---

## The canonical 4-move structure of empirical audit abstracts

The 6 empirical abstracts (`07-DHT`, `16-DLZ`, `24-DLWW`, `24-DHXZ`, `25-DQSZ`, `26-KLYY`) share a stable **4-move backbone**, occasionally extended to 5 with an extra finding or implication.

```
MOVE 1: SETUP / GAP                     [1 sentence]
        ("Prior research finds X. However, ...")
        ("A large body of research..." → gap)
        ("We develop the concept of..." for new-construct papers)

MOVE 2: PREDICTION / DESIGN             [1–2 sentences]
        ("We examine / predict / hypothesize / propose that...")
        ("We test using [data/setting]" — when not folded into MOVE 3)

MOVE 3: MAIN FINDING                    [1–2 sentences, sometimes 3]
        ("We find that...")
        ("Consistent with our prediction, we find...")

MOVE 4: COROBORATION / SECONDARY        [1 sentence] ★ optional
        ("Consistent with a causal relation...")
        ("The relation is stronger for...")
        ("Also consistent with X, we find...")

MOVE 5: IMPLICATION / TAKEAWAY          [1 sentence] ★ frequent
        ("Overall, our findings suggest...")
        ("Collectively, our findings offer...")
        ("Our findings are consistent with...")
```

### Frequency matrix

| Paper | M1 Setup/Gap | M2 Prediction | M3 Finding | M4 Corroboration | M5 Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 07-DHT | ✓ ("draw on…literature") | ✓ (implicit via M1) | ✓✓ (two findings) | — | — |
| 16-DLZ | — | — | ✓✓ (lead with finding) | ✓ ("Also consistent with…") | ✓ ("Taken together…") |
| 24-DLWW | ✓ ("Prior research…") | ✓ ("we examine…") + tension | ✓ ("We find…") | — | ✓ ("Our findings are consistent with…") |
| 24-DHXZ | ✓ ("We develop the concept…") | ✓ (folded into M3 lead) | ✓ ("We find…") | ✓✓ (endogeneity + cross-section) | ✓ ("Overall, our findings suggest…") |
| 25-DQSZ | ✓ ("Prior research concludes…However…") | ✓ ("We predict…") + ("test using Chinese data") | ✓ ("We find…") | ✓✓ (causal + mechanism) | ✓ ("Overall, by examining…") |
| 26-KLYY | ✓ ("Building on…we examine…") | ✓ ("we propose…") | ✓ ("Consistent with our prediction, we find…") | ✓✓ (cross-section + dark side) | ✓ ("Collectively…") |

**Key observation:** The 4 most recent abstracts (`24-DLWW`, `24-DHXZ`, `25-DQSZ`, `26-KLYY`) all use the same **5-move pattern** with explicit setup/gap, prediction, finding, corroboration, implication. This is the modern JAE convention. The 2007 and 2016 papers use looser structures.

**Median: ~150 words. Range: 99–165.**

The 150-word target is well-supported. Modern JAE abstracts (`24-`, `25-`, `26-`) consistently hit 145–165 words; older `07-DHT` is shorter (99) but also less informative. **Do not exceed 175 words** for JAE; TAR allows up to ~200.

Sentence count is **5–8**, with a strong mode at **6–7**.

## MOVE 1 — Setup / Gap (opening sentence)

### The 5 archetypes of opening sentences

| Archetype | Frequency | Verbatim example | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A. Prior-research-contradiction** | 2/6 | "Prior research finds that auditors' social connections with their clients harms audit quality." (`24-DLWW`) | When you contrast or refine a prior result |
| **B. Prior-research-conclusion-then-but** | 1/6 | "Prior research concludes that tax-expert auditors facilitate tax aggressiveness. However, these studies examine auditors who also provide non-audit tax services to their clients, creating conflicting incentives." (`25-DQSZ`) | When you overturn a prior finding by isolating a confound |
| **C. Theory/literature-bridge ("Building on…")** | 1/6 | "Building on economic theories of cultural transmission, we examine how audit partners' cultural trust influences audit outcomes." (`26-KLYY`) | When importing theory from outside accounting |
| **D. New-construct-development ("We develop…")** | 1/6 | "We develop the concept of auditor industry range as the extent to which an auditor has experiences in auditing clients from different industries, and we link this construct to auditor performance, drawing on prior research in psychology and cognitive science." (`24-DHXZ`) | When the contribution is a new measure or construct |
| **E. Lead-with-finding** | 1/6 | "We find that auditors of more conservative clients charge lower fees, issue fewer going concern opinions, and resign less frequently…" (`16-DLZ`) | TAR style; can be effective when finding is the main pitch |
| **F. Method-first ("We draw on…")** | 1/6 | "We draw on the investor protection literature to identify structural factors in the financial reporting environment that are likely to explain cross-country differences in the information content of annual earnings announcements." (`07-DHT`) | Older JAE style; defensible but less common in 2024+ |

### Modern JAE default (2024+)

The modal opening for a partner-trait or auditor-trait JAE abstract is **Archetype A or B** — set up the prior literature in 1 sentence, then pivot. Examples:

> `24-DLWW`: "Prior research finds that auditors' social connections with their clients harms audit quality. We examine auditors' social connections with members of their clients' business community, a setting in which auditors' connections may improve audit quality." [Sentence 1: setup. Sentence 2: pivot.]

> `25-DQSZ`: "Prior research concludes that tax-expert auditors facilitate tax aggressiveness. However, these studies examine auditors who also provide non-audit tax services to their clients, creating conflicting incentives." [Sentence 1: prior. Sentence 2: confound exposure.]

### Forbidden openings

The corpus contains **zero** instances of:
- Real-world anecdote ("In 2024, an audit failure at…")
- Policy motivation ("Audit quality matters because…")
- Broad claim ("Auditing is the cornerstone of…")
- Dictionary-style construct ("Audit quality is defined as…") — except in `14-DZ` which is a review
- "This paper sheds light on…" or "This paper investigates the question of…"
- Question form ("How does X affect Y?")
- "Recent regulatory changes in [country]…"

## MOVE 2 — Prediction / Design

### Standard verbs

| Verb form | Frequency | Example |
|---|---|---|
| "We examine…" | 4/6 | "We examine auditors' social connections…" (`24-DLWW`); "we examine how audit partners' cultural trust influences audit outcomes" (`26-KLYY`) |
| "We predict…" | 1/6 | "We predict that tax-expert auditors, who do not provide non-audit tax services, reduce tax aggressiveness…" (`25-DQSZ`) |
| "We propose that…" | 1/6 | "we propose that audit partners from trusting cultures are more likely to rely on management's assertions…" (`26-KLYY`) |
| "We hypothesize that…" | 0/6 in abstracts (it's reserved for the body) | — |
| "We develop the concept of… and link this construct to…" | 1/6 | "We develop the concept of auditor industry range…" (`24-DHXZ`) |
| "We test using [data]" | 2/6 | "We test the effects of auditors' network connections on audit quality using data from China…" (`24-DLWW`); "We test our prediction using Chinese data…" (`25-DQSZ`) |

**Note:** "Hypothesize" and "H1/H2" notation appear in the introduction's hypothesis-development block, **not** in the abstract. Abstracts use "predict," "propose," or simply "examine."

### Optional tension cue (1/6)

`24-DLWW` adds a tension cue mid-abstract:

> "While social ties within a client's business community should improve auditor competency, they also threaten auditor independence."

This signals theoretical sophistication in 1 sentence. Use sparingly — only when both directions of the prediction are theoretically defensible.

## MOVE 3 — Main Finding

### Lead-sentence formulas

| Formula | Frequency | Verbatim example |
|---|---|---|
| **"We find that…"** (bare) | 5/6 | "We find that auditors with strong local business and government connections deliver higher quality audits…" (`24-DLWW`) |
| **"Consistent with our prediction, we find…"** | 1/6 | "Consistent with our prediction, we find that audit partners from trusting cultures commit fewer Type I errors when issuing going concern opinions, without significantly increasing Type II errors." (`26-KLYY`) |
| **"We find that…"** + restated subject | 2/6 | "We find that auditors of more conservative clients charge lower fees, issue fewer going concern opinions, and resign less frequently…" (`16-DLZ`) |
| **"…we find that, on average, [X]"** | 1/6 | "We also find that, on average, earnings announcements are more informative in countries with strong investor protection institutions." (`07-DHT`) |

**The bare "We find that…" dominates.** "Consistent with our hypothesis…" / "As hypothesized…" — heavy in introductions — appears only once in the abstract corpus (`26-KLYY`). Abstracts tend toward the more declarative "We find…"

### Triple-finding format (TAR style, 16-DLZ)

`16-DLZ` packs three outcomes into the lead sentence: "auditors of more conservative clients charge lower fees, issue fewer going concern opinions, and resign less frequently." This is efficient and TAR-typical. Modern JAE prefers one main finding plus corroboration in separate sentences.

## MOVE 4 — Corroboration / Secondary findings

### Standard moves

| Move type | Frequency | Verbatim example |
|---|---|---|
| **Causal-relation language** | 1/6 | "Consistent with a causal relation, a decrease in tax rates, which reduces clients' incentives to be tax aggressive, weakens the effect of tax-expertise on tax aggressiveness." (`25-DQSZ`) |
| **Cross-sectional heterogeneity** | 3/6 | "the relation is stronger for more complex clients, in more uncertain environments, and for auditors with more years of audit experience." (`24-DHXZ`) |
| **Mechanism / sub-finding** | 2/6 | "Moreover, tax-expert auditors attenuate the type of tax aggressiveness that results in tax-related misstatements." (`25-DQSZ`); "The reduction in Type I errors is primarily found when audit partners normally tend to be more conservative, and it is attenuated when management is less trustworthy." (`26-KLYY`) |
| **Endogeneity caveat / mitigation** | 1/6 | "We conduct an extensive set of analyses to mitigate the concern that our results are driven by endogenous matching between auditors and clients." (`24-DHXZ`) |
| **"Dark-side" / cost finding** | 1/6 | "At the same time, audit partners from trusting cultures are also associated with more within-GAAP earnings management, suggesting that increased trust entails a cost." (`26-KLYY`) |
| **Falsification / additional consistency** | 1/6 | "Also consistent with conservatism reducing auditor business risk, we find that client conservatism is associated with fewer lawsuits against auditors and with fewer client restatements." (`16-DLZ`) |

**Connectives:** "Moreover," "Also consistent with," "At the same time," "Consistent with a causal relation," "The relation is stronger for…"

### Hard rule

Modern JAE abstracts in this corpus all (4/4 of `24-`/`25-`/`26-`) include **at least one corroboration sentence**. The pattern is: 1 main finding → 1–2 corroboration sentences → 1 implication.

## MOVE 5 — Implication / Closing sentence

### Closing sentence patterns

| Pattern | Frequency | Verbatim example |
|---|---|---|
| **"Overall, [findings/we] suggest [interpretation]…"** | 2/6 | "Overall, our findings suggest that an auditor's diverse experiences in different industries can enhance audit quality." (`24-DHXZ`); "Overall, by examining auditors who do not provide non-audit tax services, we find that tax-expert auditors curb tax aggressiveness, contrary to prior research." (`25-DQSZ`) |
| **"Collectively, our findings offer…"** | 1/6 | "Collectively, our findings offer new insights into how cultural trust affects the assurance of accounting information." (`26-KLYY`) |
| **"Our findings are consistent with…"** | 1/6 | "Our findings are consistent with the improved competency that arises from auditors' business connections outweighing the potential costs of impaired auditor independence." (`24-DLWW`) |
| **"Taken together, our results are consistent with…"** | 1/6 | "Taken together, our results are consistent with auditors viewing client conservatism as an important determinant of engagement risk that, in turn, affects auditor-client contracting decisions." (`16-DLZ`) |
| **No closing implication; ends on finding** | 1/6 | (`07-DHT` — older JAE style ends with "We also find…") |

### Two valid closing rhetorics

1. **Theoretical-resolution closing** — restate which mechanism wins. Used in `24-DLWW`, `16-DLZ`. Best when the introduction featured tension.
2. **Contribution closing** — restate the contribution as new insight. Used in `26-KLYY`, `24-DHXZ`, `25-DQSZ`. Best for partner-trait or new-construct papers.

### Forbidden closings

- "Future research should…" (no abstract in corpus does this)
- "Our results have implications for [practitioners/regulators/standard-setters]." (`16-DLZ` body keywords mention standard-setters but the abstract does NOT make this move)
- Restating the research question ("In summary, this paper asked whether…")
- "Our findings should be useful to…" (this DOES appear in `16-DLZ` after the implication, but it's the only instance and reads as TAR house style — avoid for JAE)

## Magnitude reporting in abstracts

**This is the single biggest difference between intros and abstracts.**

| Paper | Quantitative magnitude in abstract? | Form |
|---|---|---|
| 07-DHT | ✗ | "over 50,000 announcements" — sample size, not effect magnitude |
| 16-DLZ | ✗ | None |
| 24-DLWW | ✗ | "data from China" — no effect size |
| 24-DHXZ | ✗ | None |
| 25-DQSZ | ✗ | None |
| 26-KLYY | ✗ | None |

**Frequency: 0/6.** Modern audit-research abstracts in JAE/TAR do **not** report numerical effect magnitudes. Sample size sometimes appears (`07-DHT`: "over 50,000"), but coefficient sizes, percentage-point changes, and standard-deviation framings are reserved for the **introduction** (Block 4 of `intro_patterns.md`).

This is the **opposite convention** from the introduction. Intros require numerical magnitudes; abstracts forbid them. Don't violate this — it makes the abstract feel like a poorly compressed intro.

**Acceptable in abstracts:**
- Sample period or country count if essential ("data from 26 countries")
- Sample size if it's a striking number ("over 50,000")
- "fewer," "lower," "higher" — directional language only

| Verb | Total uses | Move(s) where used |

| **draw on / build on** | 3 | M1 |

| **shed light on** | 0 | (banned) |
| **investigate** | 0 in the abstracts (used in intros) |

### Recommended verb sequence template

```
M1: [Prior research finds / We draw on / We develop / Building on…]
M2: [We examine / predict / propose…]
M3: [We find that…]
M4: [Moreover, / Also consistent with / The relation is stronger for…]
M5: [Overall / Collectively / Our findings are consistent with / Taken together…]
```

## Differences across journals

### JAE (5/7 corpus papers)

- Length: 145–165 words modal
- Move structure: explicit 5-move (setup → prediction → finding → corroboration → implication)
- Tension acknowledged in 1 sentence (when applicable)
- China-data justification can appear in 1 phrase ("test using Chinese data")
- Final sentence almost always begins with "Overall," "Collectively," or "Our findings are consistent with"
- Zero numerical magnitudes
- No "implications for practitioners/regulators" line

### TAR (1/7 — `16-DLZ`)

- Length: 130 words (slightly shorter)
- Move structure looser; opens with finding ("We find that…") rather than setup
- Triple-finding format ("auditors charge lower fees, issue fewer GCOs, and resign less frequently")
- Includes a "should be of interest to…" line: *"Our findings should be of interest to auditors who actively manage client risk and to standard-setters who recently dropped conservatism…"*
- Includes Keywords + Data Availability lines (TAR house style)

### JAR (none in corpus)

Not represented; based on JAR house style elsewhere, expect: similar to JAE in length and structure, may include "Data Availability" line like TAR.

### Review article exception (`14-DZ`)

```
"We define higher audit quality as greater assurance of high financial reporting quality.
 Researchers use many proxies for audit quality, with little guidance on choosing among them.
 We provide a framework for systematically evaluating their unique strengths and weaknesses.
 Because it is inextricably intertwined with financial reporting quality, audit quality also depends on firms' innate characteristics and financial reporting systems.
 Our review of the models commonly used to disentangle these constructs suggests the need for better conceptual guidance.
 Finally, we urge more research on the role of auditor and client competency in driving audit quality."
```

Structure: define → describe-state-of-literature → state-contribution → caveat → call-for-research. **Do not use as a template for empirical papers** — it is a review-article abstract.

## Forbidden patterns — what's NOT in any corpus abstract

A scan of all 7 abstracts confirms the following are **absent**:

- ❌ Quantitative effect sizes (% change, std-dev, decile shifts) — abstracts have ZERO numerical magnitudes
- ❌ "This paper sheds light on…" / "This paper contributes by…" / "This paper investigates…"
- ❌ Question form ("How does X affect Y?")
- ❌ Citations to prior work (no "(Smith, 2020)" in any abstract — citations begin in §1)
- ❌ Footnotes within the abstract
- ❌ "Future research should…"
- ❌ Marketing adjectives: "novel," "unique," "important," "significant" (statistical sense), "robust" (used as adjective for findings)
- ❌ Hypothesis numbering ("H1," "H2") — reserved for body
- ❌ Methods detail beyond data setting ("we use OLS / probit / fixed effects" — none of this in abstracts)
- ❌ Acronyms without expansion on first use (`24-DHXZ` says "audit adjustment" not "AA")
- ❌ Author names ("DeFond and Zhang (2014) argue…" — no in-abstract citations)
- ❌ Tables/figures references ("As shown in Table 1…")
- ❌ Direct quotes or epigraphs (the `24-DHXZ` epigraph is BEFORE Section 1, not in the abstract)
- ❌ "Significant" as a statistical hedge ("the effect is statistically significant")
- ❌ "We propose a model where…" — abstracts of empirical papers don't propose theory
- ❌ "Our paper is the first to…" — first-claim language

## Annotated example: the closest analogue (`26-KLYY` Khurana et al. 2026)

Audit-partner-trait paper using a culturally-driven measure linked to audit outcomes — structurally the closest to a typical Chinese-data partner-trait paper.

**Verbatim abstract (154 words, 6 sentences):**

> [M1] "Building on economic theories of cultural transmission, we examine how audit partners' cultural trust influences audit outcomes."
>
> [M2] "Based on the 'presumptive doubt' perspective of professional skepticism, we propose that audit partners from trusting cultures are more likely to rely on management's assertions, while still exercising a high degree of caution and not naively trusting management."
>
> [M3] "Consistent with our prediction, we find that audit partners from trusting cultures commit fewer Type I errors when issuing going concern opinions, without significantly increasing Type II errors."
>
> [M4a] "The reduction in Type I errors is primarily found when audit partners normally tend to be more conservative, and it is attenuated when management is less trustworthy."
>
> [M4b] "At the same time, audit partners from trusting cultures are also associated with more within-GAAP earnings management, suggesting that increased trust entails a cost."
>
> [M5] "Collectively, our findings offer new insights into how cultural trust affects the assurance of accounting information."

### Move-by-move annotation

| Sentence | Move | Function | Sentence count |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1: "Building on…we examine…" | M1 | Theory-grounded setup + RQ | 1 |
| 2: "Based on…we propose that…" | M2 | Theoretical framing + prediction (with built-in tension: "still exercising caution") | 1 |
| 3: "Consistent with our prediction, we find…" | M3 | Headline finding, framed in Type I/Type II language | 1 |
| 4: "The reduction in Type I errors is primarily found when…" | M4a | Cross-sectional heterogeneity (where the effect concentrates) | 1 |
| 5: "At the same time…suggesting that increased trust entails a cost." | M4b | Dark-side finding — keeps the abstract honest | 1 |
| 6: "Collectively, our findings offer new insights…" | M5 | Implication closing | 1 |

### Why this works as a template

1. **Setup is one sentence**, theory-bridging without rehashing the literature.
2. **Prediction includes the tension** ("more likely to rely…while still exercising caution") — sophisticated in 1 sentence.
3. **Finding is direction-only** ("fewer Type I errors") — no magnitudes.
4. **Two corroboration sentences** — heterogeneity + dark side. The dark side is non-negotiable for partner-trait papers in modern JAE.
5. **Implication closes with theory-level contribution**, not policy.

For a paper on auditor early-life integrity exposure → audit quality, this is the structural template to mirror.

## Annotated example: empirical China-data analogue (`24-DLWW` DeFond et al. 2024)

**Verbatim abstract (150 words, 7 sentences):**

> [M1a] "Prior research finds that auditors' social connections with their clients harms audit quality."
>
> [M1b] "We examine auditors' social connections with members of their clients' business community, a setting in which auditors' connections may improve audit quality."
>
> [M2a-tension] "While social ties within a client's business community should improve auditor competency, they also threaten auditor independence."
>
> [M2b-design] "We test the effects of auditors' network connections on audit quality using data from China, where data on social connections and individual auditors are available."
>
> [M2c-China] "Auditors' business connections should be particularly beneficial in a relational economy like China, where clients heavily rely on social networks for contracting."
>
> [M3] "We find that auditors with strong local business and government connections deliver higher quality audits as evidenced by fewer financial irregularities among their clients."
>
> [M5] "Our findings are consistent with the improved competency that arises from auditors' business connections outweighing the potential costs of impaired auditor independence."

### Note: this abstract spends 3 sentences on M2 (tension + setting), 1 on M3, and 0 on M4 (no separate corroboration). This is a "rich-prediction, headline-finding" variant — valid but less common than the 26-KLYY shape. Pick the shape that matches your paper's strongest selling point: rich theory or rich findings.

## Self-audit checklist

Before submitting your abstract, verify:

- [ ] **Length is 130–170 words** (target 150). Strict ceiling 175 for JAE; 200 for TAR.
- [ ] **Sentence count is 5–8** with strong mode at 6–7.
- [ ] **First sentence opens INSIDE the literature** — Archetype A/B/C/D, not motivation rhetoric or "this paper."
- [ ] **Setup sentence is at most 2 sentences** before the pivot.
- [ ] **Prediction uses "we examine / predict / propose"** — NOT "we hypothesize" (reserved for body).
- [ ] **Tension acknowledged in 1 sentence** if the prediction has a natural counter-direction.
- [ ] **"We find that…"** appears at least once as a finding lead-in.
- [ ] **Zero numerical magnitudes.** No %, no std-dev, no coefficient sizes, no decile shifts. Sample-size mentions are OK if essential.
- [ ] **At least one corroboration sentence** (heterogeneity, mechanism, dark side, or causal-relation cue) — required for modern JAE.
- [ ] **Closing sentence begins with "Overall," "Collectively," "Our findings are consistent with," or "Taken together"** — NOT "future research" or "implications for practitioners."
- [ ] **No citations** to prior work in the abstract body.
- [ ] **No hypothesis numbers (H1, H2)**, no acronyms-without-expansion, no banned verbs (`shed light`, `prove`, `demonstrate definitively`), no marketing adjectives (`novel`, `unique`, `important`).
- [ ] **No methods detail** beyond data setting (`Chinese data`, `1995–2002`, `over 50,000 observations`).
- [ ] **For partner-trait or trait-on-AQ papers**: include either a tension cue (M2) or a dark-side / cost finding (M4b) — modern JAE expects one.
- [ ] **For China-data papers**: 1 phrase or 1 sentence justifying China is sufficient. Do not spend 3 sentences here unless following the `24-DLWW` shape with strong reason.
- [ ] **Final sentence restates contribution at theory level** (KLYY) **or at finding-resolution level** (DLWW), NOT at policy level.

## Quick-reference template for a partner-trait audit-quality paper

Fill in the brackets for an abstract in the dominant modern-JAE shape:

```
[M1: Building on / Drawing on / Prior research finds] [theory or literature on TRAIT].
[Pivot sentence: We examine how [TRAIT of audit partners / signatory auditors] influences [audit outcome].]

[M2: Based on / Drawing on [theoretical mechanism], we propose / predict that
     [direction of effect, with embedded tension if applicable].]

[M3: Consistent with our prediction, we find that [SUBJECT] [outcome direction]
     [outcome variable, plain language — no magnitude].]

[M4a: The [effect / relation] is [stronger / attenuated] [in setting X / for clients Y / when Z].]

[M4b (optional, recommended): At the same time / Moreover, [TRAIT] is also associated with
     [secondary or counter outcome], suggesting [interpretation].]

[M5: Collectively / Overall / Taken together, our findings [theoretical contribution
     statement — what we learn about TRAIT, audit quality, or the mechanism].]
```

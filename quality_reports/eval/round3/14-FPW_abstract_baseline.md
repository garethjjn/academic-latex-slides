Prior research treats audit quality largely at the level of the individual client, leaving open how an auditor shapes financial reporting across its broader clientele. We examine whether the shared "audit style" of a Big 4 firm, defined as the firm's unique internal rules for applying GAAP across its clients, increases the comparability of clients' reported earnings. We propose that two firms audited by the same Big 4 firm should report more comparably than two firms audited by different Big 4 firms, although client-specific reporting choices could instead dominate any auditor-level influence. Using pairwise comparisons of accruals and earnings for U.S. public companies over 1987 to 2011, we find that shared audit style is associated with greater within-auditor comparability of reported earnings. The effect is stronger where audit-style influence should be larger, such as among client pairs sharing the same audit office. Collectively, our findings suggest that audit firms exert a systematic, style-driven influence on the comparability of the financial statements they audit.

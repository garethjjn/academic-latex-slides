Prior research treats Big 4 audit firms as largely interchangeable suppliers of a homogeneous service. We examine whether each Big 4 firm instead imposes a distinctive "audit style," a unique internal set of rules for applying GAAP across its clientele, and whether this style makes the reported earnings of clients audited by the same firm more comparable. We propose that a shared style induces clients to translate similar economic events into similar accounting numbers; however, client-level differences in operations and reporting incentives could swamp any auditor-driven commonality. Using U.S. public companies over 1987 to 2011, and measuring comparability across firm-pairs, we find that pairs of clients audited by the same Big 4 firm exhibit more comparable accruals and earnings than pairs audited by different Big 4 firms. The effect is stronger in settings where audit-style influence should be more pronounced. Collectively, our findings suggest that auditor identity is a determinant of financial statement comparability, an output dimension of audit quality that prior research has largely overlooked.

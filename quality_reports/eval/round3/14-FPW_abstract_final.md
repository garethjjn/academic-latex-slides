Prior research treats the choice of a Big 4 auditor as a determinant of audit quality but says little about how a given audit firm shapes the comparability of its clients' financial statements. We examine whether each Big 4 firm imposes a distinctive "audit style," a unique internal set of rules for implementing GAAP, such that two firms audited by the same Big 4 firm report earnings more comparably than two firms audited by different Big 4 firms. We propose that a shared style harmonizes the exercise of accounting discretion across an auditor's clientele; however, because clients differ in their underlying economics, common audit style need not translate into more comparable reported earnings. Using pairwise comparisons of accruals and earnings for U.S. public companies over 1987 to 2011, we find that sharing the same Big 4 auditor is associated with greater comparability of reported earnings. The effect is stronger where audit-style influence should be more pronounced, such as among clients audited by the same office. Collectively, our findings reveal how the audit firm operates as a source of cross-client uniformity in accounting discretion, shaping the comparability of the financial reporting it assures.
